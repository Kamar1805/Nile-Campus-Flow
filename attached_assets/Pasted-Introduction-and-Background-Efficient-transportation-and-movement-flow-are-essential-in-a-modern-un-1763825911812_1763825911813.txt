Introduction and Background
Efficient transportation and movement flow are essential in a modern university environment, supporting punctuality, safety, academic productivity, administrative coordination, and overall campus experience. At Nile University of Nigeria, students, staff, and visitors rely on smooth mobility across campus for attending lectures, participating in academic activities, accessing services, and managing daily schedules. However, the university currently experiences frequent traffic congestion, delays at entry points, overcrowding during peak hours, and limited visibility into real-time traffic conditions. These issues are worsened by rapid population growth, constrained road networks, and insufficient gate processing capacity. As a result, movement across campus traffic becomes slow and unpredictable, causing lateness, increased stress, safety risks, and inefficiencies in academic operations.
Problem Statement
The Nile University community faces persistent challenges with traffic congestion and limited visibility into real-time movement conditions across campus, resulting in delays, overcrowded entry points, and inefficient mobility during peak periods. These issues stem from factors such as rapid population growth, inadequate road capacity, slow gate processing, lack of automated monitoring tools, and the absence of a system that provides timely traffic alerts and guidance. The situation is further compounded by limited coordination between students, staff, and security personnel, making it difficult to manage congestion proactively or redirect movement when necessary. As a result, commuters experience lateness, safety concerns, reduced productivity, and heightened frustration. There is a clear need for an integrated solution that improves traffic awareness, provides real-time notifications, enhances gate and route coordination, and supports smoother mobility across the university.
Project Objectives
1. 	Deliver real-time traffic notifications across the campus to ensure timely movement updates for students, staff, and visitors.
 
2. 	Develop a centralized monitoring dashboard that provides security and administrators with live traffic data, alerts, and analytics for informed decision-making.
 
3. 	Ensure system scalability and long-term reliability by designing the solution to support future population growth, additional sensors, and expanded road networks
 
4. 	Reduce congestion at campus entry points by implementing a system that streamlines gate traffic flow and improves processing efficiency.
5. Enhance commuter experience by providing users with accurate route and peak-hour guidance to minimize delays and improve punctuality.

6. Replace manual record-keeping with a reliable, digital database for driver and vehicle information.

7. Enhance communication between traffic management officers, students, and staff through timely notifications.

8. To maintain a centralized database of vehicles, drivers, and parking activities.

9. To improve mobility efficiency through a smart traffic management system that integrates campus mobility app and a hybrid teaching system.

10. Provide accurate shuttle timetables and live arrival estimates.

11. Generate daily and weekly traffic reports for management review.





Scope of the System
SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
In Scope
The project will encompass the following key components:
·   	Comprehensive wireless network redesign and deployment, ensuring full coverage, improved performance, and future scalability.
·   	Upgrade of core network infrastructure, including switches, routers, and the fiber-optic backbone to support high-speed and resilient connectivity.
·   	Deployment of advanced network management and monitoring systems to enhance visibility, control, and troubleshooting efficiency.
·   	Strengthening of network security infrastructure, integrating modern security protocols, firewalls, and access control mechanisms.
·   	Pilot implementation and performance testing to validate design decisions, discover issues early, and optimize performance before full rollout.
·   	Staff training and development of technical documentation to ensure smooth operations and long-term maintainability.
·   	Seamless integration with existing institutional systems, maintaining compatibility and avoiding major disruptions.
 
Out of Scope
The following elements fall outside the boundaries of this project:
·   	Procurement of end-user devices such as laptops, tablets, and smartphones.
·   	General building electrical upgrades, except where minor adjustments are necessary for supporting network equipment.
·   	Application-level software, including learning management systems or other third-party applications.
·   	Telephony and voice communication services, which will be addressed under a separate initiative.
·   	Campus-wide security camera systems, which are part of a related but independently managed project.
 
Limitations
The undertaking must operate within the following constraints:
·   	Existing structural conditions of campus buildings, which may limit ideal placement or cabling options.
·   	Budgetary limitations, requiring a phased deployment strategy to distribute costs and reduce financial strain.
·   	Requirement for continuous campus operations, meaning network upgrades must be executed with minimal downtime.
·   	Limited availability of specialized network engineering expertise in the local market, potentially extending timelines.
·      Vendor dependency and supply-chain uncertainties, which may impact procurement and scheduling.


Requirements Analysis
This section defines the key requirements needed to develop the proposed Smart Vehicle Entry/Exit Authorization System (SVEAS). It outlines the business requirements that capture the overall goals of the project, followed by the stakeholder requirements that reflect the needs and expectations of users and administrators. The system requirements translate these needs into essential system capabilities, while the solution requirements provide detailed functional and non-functional specifications that guide system design and performance standards.
Business Requirements
BR-01: Automate Gate Access Control to eliminate manual checks, reduce human error, and accelerate the entry/exit process for authorized vehicles.
BR-02: Enhance Campus Security by maintaining a precise, real-time audit trail of all vehicle movements across all university gates.
BR-03: Alleviate Traffic Congestion by significantly reducing vehicle waiting times, especially during peak hours.
BR-04: Improve Operational Efficiency for security personnel and administrators through centralized monitoring and control.
BR-05: Provide a Scalable and Sustainable Solution that can grow with the university and integrate with future security and traffic management systems.
BR-06: Ensure Regulatory Compliance by adhering to data protection laws and university policies regarding the collection and use of personal data.
Stakeholder Descriptions and Requirements
1. Security Officers
Description: Personnel stationed at university gates responsible for monitoring vehicle access and ensuring security.
Stakeholder Requirements:
SR-01: A simple, fast interface (e.g., tablet or terminal) to verify vehicles and view entry/exit status.
SR-02: Ability to manually override the automated system to grant or deny access during emergencies or system failures.
SR-03: Real-time alerts for unauthorized access attempts or invalid credentials.
SR-04: Access to a daily log of vehicle activity at their gate.
2. System Administrators
Description: IT personnel responsible for system configuration, user management, and overall technical health.
Stakeholder Requirements:
SR-05: A centralized dashboard for registering users, managing vehicle data, and assigning RFID tags/QR codes.
SR-06: Tools to monitor the status of all gate hardware (Raspberry Pi, RFID readers) in real-time.
SR-07: Ability to configure user roles, access permissions, and system settings.
SR-08: Access to comprehensive logs and the ability to generate detailed reports on traffic flow and security events.
3. Students & Staff (Vehicle Owners)
Description: Primary users of the vehicle access system who require seamless entry and exit.
Stakeholder Requirements:
SR-09: Frictionless, automatic gate access using an assigned RFID tag or QR code without needing to stop.
SR-10: A self-service portal to view their access history and update their own vehicle information (e.g., license plate).
SR-11: Notification in case of access issues (e.g., expired tag, invalid credentials).
4. Visitors
Description: Temporary users such as guests, contractors, and delivery personnel who require one-time or short-term access.
Stakeholder Requirements:
SR-12: Ability to pre-register online for a temporary, time-bound access pass (QR code).
SR-13: Clear signage and instructions on how to use the temporary pass at the gate.
5. University Management
Description: Decision-makers responsible for institutional security, budgeting, and strategic planning.
Stakeholder Requirements:
SR-14: Assurance that the system enhances campus security and operational efficiency.
SR-15: Access to high-level reports demonstrating traffic patterns, system utilization, and return on investment.
SR-16: A solution that is cost-effective, scalable, and aligns with the university's long-term digital transformation goals.
System Requirements
1. Access Control & Authorization Requirements
SR-01: The system shall authenticate vehicles in real-time using RFID tags or QR codes.
SR-02: The system shall automatically trigger the gate mechanism to open upon successful authorization.
SR-03: The system shall support a manual override function accessible to security officers.
SR-04: The system shall deny access and log the event for any unauthorized or invalid credential.
2. User & Vehicle Management Requirements
SR-05: The system shall allow administrators to register, edit, and deactivate user accounts (students, staff, visitors).
SR-06: The system shall allow the assignment and management of unique RFID tags and digital QR codes linked to specific vehicles and users.
SR-07: The system shall allow users to update their own vehicle details via a self-service portal.
3. Logging & Reporting Requirements
SR-08: The system shall record every entry and exit attempt, including timestamp, user/vehicle ID, gate location, and authorization status.
SR-09: The system shall provide a dashboard for administrators to view real-time logs and generate historical reports on traffic flow and security incidents.
4. Integration & Hardware Requirements
SR-10: The system shall integrate with RFID readers, QR code scanners, and gate controller relays via Raspberry Pi edge devices.
SR-11: The system shall be compatible with existing university network infrastructure for communication between gates and the central server.
SR-12: The system shall support the addition of new gates and hardware components without major architectural changes.
5. Security & Compliance Requirements
SR-13: The system shall encrypt all data transmissions between edge devices and the central server using TLS.
SR-14: The system shall enforce role-based access control (RBAC) for all administrative functions.
SR-15: The system shall comply with data retention and privacy policies as per the Nigerian Data Protection Act (NDPA).
Solution Requirements
1. Functional Requirements (FR)
Access Control
1. FR-01: The system shall read and validate RFID tag UIDs or QR code data presented at the gate.
2. FR-02: The system shall send an "open gate" signal to the relay controller upon successful authentication.
3. FR-03: The system shall provide a physical button/software switch for security officers to manually open or close the gate.
User Management
4. FR-04: The system shall allow administrators to create, read, update, and deactivate user profiles and their associated vehicles.
5. FR-05: The system shall generate and assign unique, expirable QR codes for visitor pre-registration.
6. FR-06: The system shall provide a user self-service portal for viewing access history and updating personal vehicle information.
Logging & Monitoring
7. FR-07: The system shall log all gate events (success, failure, manual override) with full contextual data.
8. FR-08: The system shall display real-time gate activity and system status on an admin dashboard.
9. FR-09: The system shall generate and export reports (PDF/CSV) for traffic analysis and audit purposes.
System Management
10. FR-10: The system shall allow administrators to monitor the online/offline status of all gate hardware.
11. FR-11: The system shall support configuration of user roles (Admin, Security Officer, User) with appropriate permissions.
2. Non-Functional Requirements (NFR)
Performance
1. NFR-01: The system shall process an authorization request and respond to the gate in under 2 seconds.
2. NFR-02: The admin dashboard shall update logged events and system status in near real-time (≤ 3-second delay).
Reliability & Availability
3. NFR-03: The core authorization and logging system shall maintain 99% uptime during campus operating hours.
4. NFR-04: The system shall implement redundancy for critical components (e.g., database, central server) to prevent single points of failure.
Security
5. NFR-05: All data in transit shall be encrypted using TLS 1.2 or higher.
6. NFR-06: The system shall implement JWT (JSON Web Tokens) for secure API authentication.
7. NFR-07: The system shall enforce strict input validation to prevent injection attacks.
Usability
8. NFR-08: The admin and security officer interfaces shall be intuitive and require minimal training for effective use.
9. NFR-09: The system shall provide clear visual and auditory feedback at the gate for access granted/denied states.
Scalability & Maintainability
10. NFR-10: The system architecture shall support scaling to a minimum of 10,000 registered vehicles and 10 separate gates.
11. NFR-11: The system shall be designed for easy maintenance, with modular components and comprehensive logging for troubleshooting.

Feasibility Study
Feasibility Study
Introduction
This feasibility study evaluates the viability of the functional requirements proposed for the Smart Traffic Flow System (STFS) at Nile University of Nigeria. It examines the technical, economic, operational, legal, and schedule dimensions to ensure that the core functions—ranging from automated vehicle authentication via RFID to dynamic gate allocation and congestion management—can be successfully developed, deployed, and maintained within the university’s growing campus environment.
Technical Feasibility
The proposed functional requirements are technically feasible, leveraging proven IoT technologies and robust software development frameworks to solve the defined traffic bottlenecks.
Hardware Compatibility: The requirement to implement automated detection is achievable using commercially available RFID readers and IoT sensors. These components can be integrated with the university's existing functioning ICT infrastructure and server capabilities without requiring entirely new foundational hardware.
Software Architecture: The development of the mobile application and admin dashboard using the proposed stack (Python/Django backend, Flutter frontend, MySQL database) is well within the capabilities of modern software engineering. High-performance tasks such as real-time queue processing are handled reliably by Redis and Celery.
System Integration: The system leverages existing connectivity. Integration with the current student/staff portal via Single Sign-On (SSO) is technically viable, ensuring a seamless flow of user data between the STFS and the university’s central database.
Economic Feasibility
The project is economically viable, with a structured budget that covers hardware acquisition, software development, and personnel training. The substantial initial investment is justified by the significant annual savings and operational efficiencies.
Cost Breakdown:
Hardware: Includes RFID readers, IoT sensors, gate automation hardware, and server upgrades.
Software: Covers developer salaries (Python/Flutter specialists), database licensing, and cloud/hosting services.
Operational & Contingency: Allocated for security staff training, system maintenance, and unforeseen deployment expenses.
Total Estimated Cost: ₦37,000,000
Return on Investment (ROI): The project is projected to save approximately ₦38,500,000 per year through reduced security staff overtime, lower fuel/maintenance costs for campus shuttles, and the avoidance of costly physical infrastructure expansion.
Payback Period: Approximately 11 to 12 months, making this a highly sound financial decision for the university.

Operational Feasibility
The system is designed to fit seamlessly into the daily operations of Nile University's security team, students, and staff.
User Adoption: Students and staff are already accustomed to using digital platforms for university services. The functional requirement for an intuitive Flutter-based mobile app ensures high adoption rates with minimal friction.
Administrative Control: The transition from manual security checks to an automated dashboard allows security personnel to focus on exceptions rather than routine access. Training sessions will ensure ease of adoption for ground staff.
Maintenance & Scalability: The system is designed to be maintained by the existing ICT team. Furthermore, the modular architecture allows the system to scale automatically as the campus population grows or as new gates are constructed.
Legal and Regulatory Feasibility
The functional requirements comply with all relevant institutional and national data regulations.
Data Protection: The system complies strictly with the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR).
Privacy & Consent: Data collection is minimised to essential metrics only (license plate, RFID tag, and timestamps). User consent is obtained via the application’s terms and conditions and during the vehicle registration process.
Security Standards: All sensitive data will be encrypted and stored securely on the university’s internal servers, ensuring protection against unauthorized access and alignment with university ICT security policies.
Schedule Feasibility
The project timeline is estimated at 6 months, which is sufficient to deliver all defined functional requirements and ensure the system is ready for the new academic session.
Months 1–2: Requirements finalisation, vendor selection for RFID/IoT hardware, and system design.
Months 3–4: Concurrent hardware installation at gates and software development (Mobile App & Backend).
Month 5: System integration, internal testing, and security staff training.
Month 6: Pilot deployment and full rollout aligned with the semester break to minimise traffic disruption. This schedule is realistic and includes buffer time for potential hardware delivery delays or integration testing.
Conclusion
The feasibility analysis confirms that the functional requirements for the Smart Traffic Flow System (STFS) are realistic and achievable. The solution is technically sound, utilizing proven RFID and IoT technologies; economically justifiable, with a payback period of less than one year; operationally sustainable for the security and ICT units; and legally compliant with NDPR regulations. Therefore, the project is recommended for immediate initiation to resolve the critical traffic congestion issues at Nile University.

Limitations of the Prototype
1. No Real Backend Integration
The prototype does not connect to an actual application server, which is planned to be Flask-based.
No real-time API communication with the RFID reader/Raspberry Pi edge devices is possible.
All actions (login, access logs, analytics) only mimic real behaviour.



2. No Database Connectivity
The system lacks integration with the central PostgreSQL database.
All vehicle logs, user data, and authorization statuses shown are mock data and cannot be persistently stored or updated.
Multi-user and multi-gate scenarios cannot be accurately tested.

3. Authentication is Simulated
The prototype uses a mock login flow for security officers and administrators.
The required JWT (JSON Web Token) authentication and Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) are not implemented.
Security mechanisms and permission checks are not verified.

4. Network Monitoring Data is Not Real
The status of gate hardware, such as the Raspberry Pi, RFID readers, and gate barriers, is simulated.
The prototype cannot provide real-time monitoring of hardware failures or operational status, failing a key visibility objective.

5. No API Integrations
External APIs (e.g., for optional SMS/Email alerts) and internal APIs (for device-to-server communication) are not functional in the prototype.
Placeholder values are used in place of real API responses.

6. Limited Administrative Functionality
The admin dashboard demonstrates the layout, but core management workflows are not functional.
Actions such as vehicle registration, user status management, and real-time alert configuration do not execute real commands.

7. No Real-Time Updates
The prototype cannot refresh automatically as a vehicle passes the gate due to the lack of a backend connection.
Live analytics (traffic load, entry/exit logs) are not supported 

8. Performance and Scalability Not Tested
The prototype does not reflect load performance under real-world traffic conditions during peak hours.
The system's scalability to support the university's entire vehicle capacity or multiple gate deployments is untested.

9. No Error Handling or System Recovery Logic
Most prototype operations assume successful execution.
The prototype does not include server-side error handling, network failure scenarios, or the required redundancy logic (NFR-04) for critical components.

10. Visual Design Still Subject to Revision
Although functional, the UI/UX is preliminary and will require further refinement during full development.
It has not yet undergone formal usability testing with security personnel or administrators.

Alignment with the Final System Requirements
The developed prototype represents a foundational step toward the complete Smart Vehicle Entry/Exit Authorization System (SVEAS). While not fully implemented, the prototype demonstrates strong alignment with the core system requirements and provides a clear pathway for scaling into the final solution.
1. Alignment with Business Requirements
The prototype visualizes the core goal of automating vehicle access control (BR-01) and enhancing security (BR-02) by providing the dashboard and verification interfaces.
2. Alignment with Stakeholder Requirements
The design incorporates distinct interfaces for Security Officers (for vehicle verification) and System Administrators(for records and management), meeting their specific monitoring and operational needs.
3. Alignment with Functional Requirements
All major functional modules—Access Control (simulating grant/deny), Logging (recording event history), and User Management—are represented by dedicated screens and interactions in the prototype.
4. Alignment with Non-Functional Requirements
The UI design prioritizes Usability (NFR-08) through a clean layout, and the architecture prepares for Reliability and Security features like WPA3 encryption.
5. Alignment with System Architecture Requirements
The prototype's structure utilizes a Frontend Layer that is ready to connect seamlessly to the planned Flask-based Backend and PostgreSQL Database Layer.
6. Alignment with Compliance & Security Requirements
The prototype supports the security requirement for Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) by enforcing separate login and view structures for different user roles.
